\begin{abstract}
We present an open-source, end-to-end pipeline for the statistical analysis
of X-ray bursts from the magnetar SGR\,1935+2154 during the April 2020
burst storm that produced the first Galactic Fast Radio Burst (FRB\,200428).
Using four archival NICER observations, 310\,738 filtered photon events,
and a 51\,790-event subsample for Bayesian Blocks segmentation, we detect
139 X-ray bursts and characterise their energy and duration distributions
using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). The burst
energies follow a power law with index $\alpha = 1.99 \pm 0.15$, and
inter-burst waiting times are highly clustered (coefficient of variation
$\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$; lognormal favoured by AIC; Weibull shape
$k = 0.44$), strongly departing from a Poisson process. By injecting the published properties of the
FRB-associated X-ray burst ($E = 7 \times 10^{39}$\,erg; Li et al.\ 2021)
into the NICER burst catalogue, we find that it is \textbf{statistically
anomalous}: it lies at the 100th percentile of the energy distribution,
$3.6\sigma$ above the population mean in log-energy space. A
self-organised criticality (SOC) test yields an inconsistency of
$2.8\sigma$ between the measured and predicted energy exponents, suggesting
that the standard SOC framework may not fully describe the burst storm.
Our results support models in which FRB emission requires an exceptionally
energetic magnetospheric event, exceeding a critical threshold not reached
by the vast majority of X-ray bursts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Magnetars are isolated neutron stars powered by the decay of ultra-strong
magnetic fields ($B \sim 10^{14}$--$10^{15}$\,G). They exhibit sporadic
bursting activity in X-rays and soft gamma-rays, with burst energies
spanning $\sim\!10^{36}$--$10^{41}$\,erg \citep{kaspi2017}. The energy
distributions of magnetar bursts typically follow power laws,
$\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}E \propto E^{-\alpha}$, with indices
$\alpha \approx 1.4$--$1.8$ \citep{gogus1999, gogus2000}, reminiscent of
self-organised criticality (SOC) --- the physics governing earthquakes,
solar flares, and avalanches \citep{bak1987, aschwanden2016}.

On 28 April 2020, SGR\,1935+2154 produced FRB\,200428, the first Fast
Radio Burst associated with a known astrophysical source
\citep{bochenek2020, chime2020}. The simultaneous detection of an X-ray
burst by Insight-HXMT \citep{li2021}, INTEGRAL \citep{mereghetti2020},
AGILE \citep{tavani2021}, and Konus-Wind \citep{ridnaia2021} confirmed
the magnetar--FRB connection. Yet SGR\,1935+2154 had emitted thousands of
X-ray bursts during this burst storm without any accompanying radio
emission, raising a fundamental question:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Was the FRB-producing burst statistically anomalous compared to the
surrounding X-ray burst population, or was it an ordinary burst that
happened to produce radio emission?}
\end{quote}

To answer this, we need a unified approach. Most existing analyses treat the X-ray storm and the radio burst separately: spectral studies focus on the broadband properties of the FRB-associated X-ray burst \citep{li2021, mereghetti2020}, while catalogues characterise the storm's temporal statistics without directly contextualising the FRB \citep{younes2020}. 

In this paper, we present an end-to-end pipeline that bridges this gap. We ingest raw NICER event files, detect bursts via Bayesian Blocks, fit the energy and waiting-time distributions, and evaluate the FRB-associated burst directly against the resulting population. We quantify the anomaly in two ways: by its rank within the observed sample, and by its consistency with the fitted power-law tail via a sample-maximum test. This dual approach helps separate bursts that are merely "extreme in the sample" from those that are genuinely "inconsistent with the model." Finally, we verify the robustness of these claims against variations in detection parameters and cross-instrument bandpass assumptions.

\subsection{Event Filtering}

We applied the following filters to the cleaned event files:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Energy selection:} PI channels 50--1000
          (approximately 0.5--10\,keV), retaining NICER's primary
          sensitivity band and rejecting optical loading and particle
          background.
    \item \textbf{GTI enforcement:} Only events falling within
          instrument-defined Good Time Intervals were retained.
\end{enumerate}
After filtering and combining all four observations, the final dataset
contains 310\,738 photon events. Because Bayesian Blocks scales poorly with
event count, we apply the change-point search to a uniform 51\,790-event
subsample while retaining the full filtered event stream for summary
statistics and light-curve visualisation.

\subsection{FRB\,200428 Timing}

The FRB occurred at 14:34:24\,UTC on 28 April 2020
\citep{chime2020, bochenek2020}. At this moment, NICER was in an orbital
gap (Earth occultation), so the X-ray counterpart was not directly
observed by NICER but was detected by Insight-HXMT \citep{li2021} and
INTEGRAL \citep{mereghetti2020}. We adopt the published properties of the
FRB-associated X-ray burst from \citet{li2021}: total isotropic energy
$E = 7 \times 10^{39}$\,erg (1--250\,keV) at a distance of 9\,kpc, and
duration $T \approx 0.5$\,s. This burst is injected into our NICER
catalogue for comparative analysis.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Bayesian Blocks Burst Detection}

Rather than using fixed time bins (which impose arbitrary temporal
resolution), we employ the Bayesian Blocks algorithm
\citep{scargle1998, scargle2013} as implemented in \texttt{astropy}. This
algorithm finds the optimal piecewise-constant representation of a
time series by maximising a fitness function penalised by model complexity.
We use a false-positive rate of $p_0 = 0.05$.

The algorithm identifies change points in the photon arrival rate. We
estimate the quiescent count rate $q$ as the 25th percentile of the
positive block-rate distribution and classify a block as a ``burst'' if
its rate exceeds $q + 3\sqrt{q}$. Adjacent burst blocks are merged, and
for each burst we record the start time, end time, duration, peak rate,
and total counts.

Burst energies are estimated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
    E = N_{\mathrm{counts}} \times \bar{E}_\gamma \times 4\pi d^2,
\end{equation}
where $N_{\mathrm{counts}}$ is the number of photons in the burst,
$\bar{E}_\gamma = 5$\,keV is the mean photon energy for NICER's bandpass,
and $d = 9$\,kpc is the source distance.

\subsection{Power-Law Fitting via MLE}

We fit the burst energy distribution using the Maximum Likelihood
Estimator for a power law with lower cutoff $x_{\min}$
\citep{clauset2009}. Specifically, for data $\{x_i\}$ with $x_i \geq x_{\min}$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\alpha} = 1 + n\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \ln\frac{x_i}{x_{\min}}\right]^{-1},
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the number of data points above $x_{\min}$. The optimal
$x_{\min}$ is selected by minimising the Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS)
statistic. We use the \texttt{powerlaw} Python package
\citep{alstott2014}, which implements the \citet{clauset2009} methodology.

\subsection{Waiting Time Analysis}

The inter-burst waiting time $\Delta t_i = t_{i+1}^{\mathrm{start}} -
t_i^{\mathrm{start}}$ is computed for consecutive bursts. For a
stationary Poisson process, waiting times are exponentially distributed
with coefficient of variation $\mathrm{CV} = \sigma/\mu = 1$.
A $\mathrm{CV} \gg 1$ indicates temporal clustering.

We fit both an exponential and a Weibull distribution:
\begin{equation}
    f(t; k, \lambda) = \frac{k}{\lambda}\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{k-1}
    \exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{k}\right],
\end{equation}
where $k < 1$ indicates clustering (sub-Poissonian) and $k = 1$ recovers
the exponential (Poisson) case.

\subsection{FRB Anomaly Test}

We quantify the anomaly of the FRB burst by computing:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Its \textbf{energy percentile} within the NICER burst population.
    \item Its \textbf{z-score} in log-energy space:
        $z = (\log E_{\mathrm{FRB}} - \langle\log E\rangle) / \sigma_{\log E}$.
    \item Whether it qualifies as an \textbf{outlier} by exceeding the
          95th percentile of the comparison sample.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Self-Organised Criticality Test}

SOC predicts three simultaneous power-law distributions
\citep{aschwanden2016}:
\begin{align}
    P(E) &\propto E^{-\alpha}, \label{eq:soc1}\\
    P(T) &\propto T^{-\beta},  \label{eq:soc2}\\
    E    &\propto T^{\delta},   \label{eq:soc3}
\end{align}
with the consistency relation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soc_relation}
    \alpha_{\mathrm{pred}} = 1 + \frac{\beta - 1}{\delta}.
\end{equation}
We fit each distribution independently and test whether
$|\alpha_{\mathrm{measured}} - \alpha_{\mathrm{pred}}|$ is consistent
with zero within the uncertainties.

Two systematic differences require explanation:

\textbf{Fewer bursts (139 vs.\ 217+).} Younes et al.\ used fixed-width
time bins and a simpler count-rate threshold, which can split single broad
bursts into multiple short events and detect faint bursts with fewer counts.
Our Bayesian Blocks algorithm with $3\sigma$ threshold and a minimum of 10
counts per burst is more conservative. Our sensitivity grid
(Section~\ref{sec:robustness}) shows that this choice does not affect the
FRB anomaly ranking.

\textbf{Steeper $\alpha$ (1.99 vs.\ 1.5).} Younes et al.\ fit fluence
($\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}F$) directly, while we convert to
isotropic-equivalent energy using a fixed $\langle E_{\gamma}\rangle$.
They also fit over $\sim$3 orders of magnitude spanning a larger dynamic
range. Our steeper index is driven by a higher $x_{\min}$ (set
automatically by the MLE procedure of \citealt{clauset2009}), which
selects a narrower tail. Our sensitivity grid shows $\alpha$ varies
from 1.57 to 1.99 across the tested parameters, bracketing the Younes
et al.\ value. This systematic variation is reported as a modelling
uncertainty throughout.

\subsection{Energy Distribution}\label{sec:energy}

The MLE fit to the 139 NICER burst energies yields a power-law index:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = 1.99 \pm 0.15 \quad (\text{systematic range: } 1.57\text{--}1.99),
\end{equation}
with an optimal lower cutoff $x_{\min} = 7.6 \times 10^{37}$\,erg
(Figure~\ref{fig:energy}). The statistical uncertainty is from the MLE;
the systematic range is from the sensitivity grid
(Section~\ref{sec:robustness}). The KS bootstrap goodness-of-fit test
(Clauset et al.\ 2009, \S3.4) yields $p = 0.67$, confirming the power-law
fit is statistically acceptable ($p > 0.1$).

\subsection{Waiting Time Distribution}\label{sec:waiting}

The inter-burst waiting times show extreme temporal clustering
(Figure~\ref{fig:waiting}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Coefficient of variation: $\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$ (Poisson: $\mathrm{CV} = 1$).
    \item Best AIC model: lognormal.
    \item Best-fit Weibull shape parameter: $k = 0.44$ ($k < 1$ indicates
          clustering).
    \item Median waiting time: 4.1\,s, but with a long tail extending to
          21\,644\,s ($\sim\!6$\,hours).
\end{itemize}
The waiting times are profoundly non-Poissonian. The measured $\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$ far exceeds the Poisson expectation of unity, and the Weibull shape $k = 0.44$ independently confirms temporal clustering. For comparison, \citet{younes2020} reported a qualitatively similar lognormal waiting-time distribution for the same burst storm using fixed-width binning. The extreme ratio between the median waiting time ($4.1$\,s) and the maximum ($21\,644$\,s) implies that the burst activity switches between rapid-fire episodes and long quiescent gaps --- behaviour expected from stress-loading and sudden release in the magnetar crust \citep{kaspi2017}.

\textbf{Rank-based result.} The FRB burst is more energetic than
\textit{every} 139 NICER-detected burst, lying at the 100th percentile
and $3.62\sigma$ above the mean in log-energy space.

\textbf{Model-based result.} However, a sample-maximum consistency test
reveals that the fitted power law ($\alpha \approx 2.0$) produces
sample maxima at least as large as the FRB energy in $\sim$40\% of
10\,000 simulated 139-burst populations. This means the FRB energy,
while extreme in the observed sample, is \textbf{consistent with the
heavy tail} of the fitted energy distribution.

These two findings are complementary, not contradictory. The FRB burst
is the most energetic event observed, but the power law predicts that
the sample maximum should occasionally reach this energy for populations
of this size. The conservative conclusion is that FRB production is
compatible with sampling the high-energy tail of the burst population.

The measured $\alpha = 1.99$ differs from the SOC-predicted
$\alpha_{\mathrm{pred}} = 2.96$ by $2.8\sigma$. While this exceeds the
$2\sigma$ level, it does not reach the conventional $3\sigma$
threshold for a definitive rejection. We interpret this as
\textbf{marginal tension} with the standard SOC framework, rather than
a conclusive rejection. Several factors may contribute to this tension:
(i)~the April 2020 storm was a transient, triggered event rather than a
quasi-static SOC system \citep{aschwanden2016}; (ii)~the energy--duration
scaling exponent $\delta$ is measured with moderate scatter ($R^2 = 0.51$);
and (iii)~$\alpha$ varies from 1.57 to 1.99 across our sensitivity grid,
which would reduce the tension for flatter indices. Many magnetar
burst studies report SOC-like behaviour without requiring exact
scaling-relation closure \citep{gogus1999, gogus2000}.

\subsection{Robustness Tests}\label{sec:robustness}

To ensure our results are not artifacts of analysis choices, we perform
five robustness checks:

\textbf{(1) Sensitivity grid.} We sweep $p_0 \in \{0.01, 0.02, 0.05,
0.10\}$ and threshold $\sigma \in \{2.0, 2.5, 3.0, 3.5\}$ using the
same pre-subsampled event list. Across all 16 combinations, the FRB
z-score ranges from 3.49 to $3.65\sigma$, confirming the ranking is
stable. However, $\alpha$ varies from 1.57 to 1.99 --- a material
systematic uncertainty that we report alongside the statistical error.

\textbf{(2) Sample-maximum test.} We draw 10\,000 synthetic 139-burst
populations from the fitted power law and find that the sample maximum
exceeds the FRB energy in $\sim$40\% of realisations, confirming
tail consistency.

\textbf{(3) Bandpass/distance sensitivity.} We test the anomaly ranking
across FRB energies from $10^{39}$\,erg (NICER-band at $d = 6$\,kpc)
to $10^{40}$\,erg (broadband at $d = 12$\,kpc). The FRB exceeds the
95th percentile for all five tested values.

\textbf{(4) Catalogue completeness.} NICER observed only 0.5\% of the
total observation window (5\,703\,s GTI across 43 intervals out of a
$1.18 \times 10^6$\,s span). Because the burst storm is highly
non-stationary ($\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$), we report only the within-GTI
burst rate (24.4\,bursts\,ks$^{-1}$) and do not extrapolate total burst
counts.

\textbf{(5) KS goodness-of-fit.} The semi-parametric bootstrap KS test
(\citealt{clauset2009}, \S3.4) yields $p = 0.67 > 0.1$, confirming the
power-law fit is statistically acceptable.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The FRB Burst: Extreme but Tail-Consistent}

Our primary result has two complementary components. First, the
FRB-associated burst lies at the 100th percentile of the NICER energy
distribution ($3.6\sigma$), making it the most energetic event in the
observed sample regardless of analysis parameters. Second, the fitted
power-law tail accommodates energies this large in $\sim$40\% of
simulated populations of the same size. Together, these imply that
FRB production is compatible with sampling the extreme high-energy tail
of the magnetar burst population, rather than requiring a fundamentally
distinct emission mechanism.

This supports models in which FRB emission requires an exceptionally
energetic magnetospheric event but does not demand physics beyond the
normal burst energy distribution. The synchrotron maser model
\citep{lyubarsky2014, metzger2019} predicts that coherent radio
emission requires a relativistic shock with sufficient Lorentz factor
--- a condition met only at the very tip of the energy distribution.
Our dual analysis provides quantitative evidence for this picture.

\subsection{Physical Origin of Burst Clustering}

The $\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$ we measure is an order of magnitude above the Poisson value, and the Weibull shape $k = 0.44$ is strikingly close to the $k \approx 0.34$ reported by \citet{lin2020} for the 2014--2015 activity window of this same source. Both values point to a burst-triggering process where events are not independent: once the crust or magnetosphere destabilises, bursts arrive in rapid succession before the system re-enters a quiescent phase.

Physically, this pattern is expected from magnetic stress accumulation in the neutron star crust. As the internal field evolves, elastic strain builds until it exceeds the breaking stress, triggering a cascade of fractures --- each fracture redistributing stress to neighbouring regions and potentially launching further bursts \citep{kaspi2017}. The Weibull $k < 1$ is a direct signature of this cascading process: the hazard rate \emph{decreases} with time since the last burst, meaning bursts are more likely to occur in clusters than at random.

Notably, repeating FRB sources such as FRB\,121102 and FRB\,20201124A show qualitatively similar non-Poissonian clustering \citep{chime2020}, which has been interpreted as evidence for a common magnetar origin. Our measurement of $\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$ for SGR\,1935+2154's X-ray bursts adds a quantitative data point to this comparison.

\subsection{Caveats and Limitations}

\textbf{Cross-instrument comparison.} The FRB burst energy
($7 \times 10^{39}$\,erg) is from Insight-HXMT (1--250\,keV;
\citealt{li2021}), while NICER burst energies are estimated in
0.5--10\,keV using a simplified counts-to-energy conversion
($E = N \times \langle E_\gamma \rangle \times 4\pi d^2 / A_{\mathrm{eff}}$
with $\langle E_\gamma \rangle = 5$\,keV). Full spectral fitting
(e.g.\ blackbody at $kT \approx 1.7$\,keV per \citealt{younes2020})
with NICER response matrices would produce more accurate absolute energies.
However, for the rank-based anomaly test (percentile and z-score), the
absolute scale cancels: the same conversion is applied to all NICER
bursts. Our bandpass sensitivity test confirms the FRB exceeds the 95th
percentile for all assumed energies from $10^{39}$ to $10^{40}$\,erg.

\textbf{Fewer bursts than Younes et al.\ (2020).} We detect 139 bursts
versus their 217+. The difference arises from our more conservative
Bayesian Blocks detection ($3\sigma$ threshold, 10-count minimum) versus
their fixed-width binning. Our sensitivity grid shows the anomaly
ranking is unchanged across all tested detection parameters
(z-score 3.49--3.65$\sigma$).

\textbf{Catalogue incompleteness.} NICER observed only 0.5\% of the
total time window. We report only the within-GTI burst rate and do not
extrap\-olate total burst counts, because the storm is profoundly
non-stationary ($\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$).

\textbf{FRB burst injection.} The FRB X-ray counterpart occurred
during a NICER orbital gap and was detected by Insight-HXMT, not NICER.
We inject its published properties as a separate catalogue entry
for comparison only. It is excluded from all distribution fits
(energy, duration, waiting time). Its presence does not bias the
NICER-derived power-law index or the SOC exponents.

\textbf{$\alpha$ systematic uncertainty.} The fitted power-law index
varies from 1.57 to 1.99 across the sensitivity grid. This systematic
range exceeds the statistical error ($\pm 0.15$) and should be
interpreted as a modelling uncertainty arising from detection parameters.

\textbf{Distance uncertainty.} The assumed distance of 9\,kpc
\citep{kothes2018} has a literature range of 6--12\,kpc. This affects
the absolute energy scale but not the within-NICER ranking. The
bandpass/distance test covers this range.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

The complete pipeline is open source and available at
\url{https://github.com/magnetar-frb-analysis}. All FITS data are publicly
available from HEASARC. Running \texttt{python run\_real\_data.py}
reproduces the full analysis --- from raw photon events to publication
figures and robustness tests --- in approximately 90 seconds.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We detected 139 X-ray bursts from SGR\,1935+2154 using Bayesian Blocks segmentation of NICER data from the April 2020 burst storm. The burst energies follow a power law with $\alpha = 1.99 \pm 0.15$ (systematic range $1.57$--$1.99$ across 16 parameter combinations), and the inter-burst waiting times are strongly clustered ($\mathrm{CV} = 11.25$, Weibull $k = 0.44$).

The FRB\,200428 X-ray counterpart ($7 \times 10^{39}$\,erg; Li et al.\ 2021) sits at the 100th percentile of the NICER energy distribution, $3.6\sigma$ above the log-energy mean. At the same time, the fitted power-law tail produces sample maxima at least this large in $\sim$40\% of Monte Carlo realisations, so the FRB energy is extreme but not inconsistent with the underlying distribution. Taken together, these two findings point to an energy-threshold picture: the burst population follows a single power law, but only events sampling the very tip of the tail --- roughly above $10^{39.5}$\,erg --- meet the conditions for coherent radio emission.

Two clear limitations should be addressed in future work. First, our energy proxy uses a flat 5\,keV conversion rather than per-burst spectral fitting with NICER response matrices; applying the \citet{younes2020} blackbody model ($kT \approx 1.7$\,keV) would sharpen the absolute energy scale. Second, the comparison between NICER-band and Insight-HXMT broadband energies is inherently cross-instrument; simultaneous multi-band coverage from AstroSat or IXPE during a future burst storm would remove this uncertainty.
